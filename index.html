Extraversion/Introversion

Extrovert

We learned an extrovert is an outgoing, overtly expressive person. We also learned that extroverts enjoy being in large group discussions. They enjoy working in open spaces and they are confident to speak to anyone. Extroverts make more friends but less strong bonds and they make decisions quickly. After learning more about extroverts we invited extroverts to larger group discussions as they perform the best when sharing their ideas will lots of people.

Introvert

We learned that an introvert is a shy, reticent person and they recharge by spending time alone. They enjoy one on one conversation and enjoy working in quiet spaces. Introverts reflect before making a decision and they share ideas promptly. Introverts are also great listeners and love to hear other peoples' ideas and will give fewer ideas compared to extroverts, but with much deeper thought. Now we do not turn away from introverts when searching for group members because they also can bring a lot to the table.

Ambivert

A person whose personality has a balance of extrovert and introvert features. We learned that an ambivert is someone who exhibits qualities of both introversion and extroversion, and can flip into either depending on their mood, context, and goals. We learned Ambiverts have lots of great traits. They're able to be flexible in a variety of situations, often knowing when to talk and when to listen.

Leadership

It is the ability to influence a group towards the achievement of goals. We learned that leaders can influence an individual. We learned to use leadership to lead a project or task in school. This can be any level of school or work, organising a study group or Volunteer/non-profit leadership. There are many different types of leadership and leadership can be narrowed down. After learning more about leadership we improved our leadership skills and are now far better at things like leading group descussions and assigning tasks to others.

Negotiation

We learned that negotiation, often known as bargaining, is a process in which two or more parties exchange products or services and attempt to agree on a rate of exchange. Negotiation can take many different shapes, and different cultures use different methods. A great process for negotiation is developing a strategy and ground rules, clarifying and justifying, bargaining and problem solving and finally closing and implementing. After expanding our knowledge about negotiation we are better at negotiating and creating a win-win situation where both parties are satisfied.

Conflicts

A conflict is when there are two parties are in disagreement. We learned that there are two different types of conflict; Cognitive Conflict is when one's beliefs, attitudes, or behaviours contradict one another. Affective conflict happens when two parties realise that their sentiments and emotions about some or all of the issues are conflicting while attempting to solve a problem together. We also learned that not all conflicts are neccesarily bad, and some conflicts can help the group achieve its objectives and improve its performance while others can obstruct a group's performance. Now when we run into conflicts we do not always see it as a bad thing and we can use conflicts positivily.
